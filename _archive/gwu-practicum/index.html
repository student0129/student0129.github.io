Macro Perspective

Where AI Stands Today

Four interconnected forces are reshaping the field right now — and understanding them will make you a sharper practitioner.

1
Scaling Hit a Wall

Brute-force model sizing is showing diminishing returns. DeepSeek proved frontier-quality reasoning can be achieved for a fraction of the cost, breaking the Big Tech compute monopoly.

2
Models Don't Actually Reason

Cognitive scientists warn that LLMsLarge Language Models: AI systems trained on vast amounts of text to predict and generate human-like language. are sophisticated pattern matchers, not logical reasoners. They fundamentally struggle with formal rules and math without additional training techniques like RLVRReinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards: A training method where the model is rewarded only when it can independently verify its own answer is correct (e.g., by running code or checking math)..

3
The Neurosymbolic Response

Because neural networks can't guarantee logical correctness, the industry is converging toward combining them with deterministic Symbolic AITraditional AI that uses explicit, human-readable rules and logic engines rather than probabilistic neural networks — guaranteeing correct outcomes for well-defined problems.: structured rules, formal verification, and math engines that enforce accuracy.

4
Data Is the New Moat

With model architectures converging, the lasting differentiator is proprietary data. The organization that fine-tunes or grounds its models on unique, high-quality domain data will outperform one relying on a generic base model — regardless of which frontier model they use.

Technical Foundations

How We Got to Large Language Models

The field of NLPNatural Language Processing: The branch of AI focused on enabling machines to understand and generate human language. went through three distinct generations before landing on the Decoder-Only architecture that powers every modern LLM.

1 · Static Vectors

Word2Vec / GloVe

Context-Free: Each word gets one fixed numerical vector regardless of context. The word "bank" always maps to the same point, even in "river bank" vs. "bank account."

2 · Encoder-Only

BERT Family

Bidirectional Context: Reads the full sentence in both directions to understand meaning in context. Excellent for classification and search — but cannot generate text.

3 · Encoder-Decoder

T5 / BART

Sequence-to-SequenceAn architecture that reads an entire input string (e.g., English) and transforms it into a new output string (e.g., French).: The gold standard for translation and summarization — encodes full input meaning, then decodes into a new output.

4 · Decoder-Only

GPT / Llama / Claude

AutoregressiveA model that generates text one token at a time, feeding each new token back into itself as input to predict the next. This loop continues until the answer is complete.: Predicts the next token, left-to-right. Massively scalable. Absorbs all prior capabilities — the dominant architecture today.

Technical Foundations

Inside the Decoder

Now that we know Decoder-Only is the dominant architecture, let's open it up. How does text actually flow through the model from input to output?

The key insight: the Transformer Block (steps 2–3) does not run once. It stacks N times — GPT-4 has ~96 layers. Each pass gives the model a deeper, richer representation of the input.

The Forward Pass

1
Token Embeddings + Position

Raw text is split into sub-words (tokensSub-word units of text. The word "unbelievable" might become three tokens: ["un", "believ", "able"]. Each becomes a number.) and each token is mapped to a high-dimensional vector. Positional encodingsMathematical tags appended to each embedding so the model knows the order of words in the sentence. are added so the model knows word order.

N×
Transformer Block (repeated N layers)

Multi-Head AttentionRunning multiple self-attention operations in parallel — each "head" learns to focus on different relationships (e.g., grammar, coreference, topic) simultaneously.: Tokens attend to each other in parallel across multiple heads, each learning different relationships.
Then: A Feed-Forward Network transforms each token's representation independently, adding non-linear reasoning capacity.

3
LogitsThe raw, unnormalized scores over the entire vocabulary produced by the final layer — one score per possible next word. → SoftmaxConverts raw Logit scores into a probability distribution (0–1) over the vocabulary. The model samples from this distribution to pick the next token. → Next Token

The final layer scores every word in the vocabulary, converts to probabilities, samples the most likely next token, appends it to the input, and loops back to step 1.

Technical Foundations

Not All Decoders Are Equal

Every major LLM is a Decoder-Only Transformer — but architectural choices made inside that structure determine speed, cost, and capability at scale.

  • Dense vs. Sparse (MoE)Mixture of Experts: Rather than activating all model parameters for every input, MoE routes each token to a small subset of specialized sub-networks, drastically reducing compute costs.: Dense models (Llama) activate every parameter for every token. MoE models (DeepSeek, Mixtral) route tokens to specialized sub-networks — far lower inference cost at the same parameter count.
  • Context Window: How much text the model can "see" at once. Jumped from 4K tokens (GPT-3) to 1M+ tokens (Gemini 1.5) via techniques like GQAGrouped-Query Attention: An optimization that shares key-value memory across attention heads, slashing the GPU RAM needed for long contexts. and RoPE scaling.
  • Attention Variant: Standard MHAMulti-Head Attention: The original full attention mechanism where every head has its own independent key-value memory — powerful but memory-heavy. vs. GQAGrouped-Query Attention: Groups multiple query heads to share a single key-value head, cutting memory use while preserving quality — used in Llama 3 and Mistral. vs. MLAMulti-head Latent Attention: DeepSeek's technique that compresses key-value pairs into a low-dimensional latent vector, enabling very long contexts at minimal memory cost. — each trading quality for memory efficiency differently.

Why These Choices Matter

These three dimensions directly determine the cost, speed, and limits of any LLM you deploy:

  • Dense vs. MoE — controls compute per token. MoE activates only a subset of parameters, so more capacity doesn't always mean higher cost.
  • Context window — how much the model can "see" at once. Larger windows consume more GPU memory, raising inference cost.
  • Attention variant — the main lever for managing that memory cost, making long contexts economically viable.
Cognitive Architecture

Reasoning Models

Standard Decoders predict the next word reactively. Reasoning models take a different approach: think of human cognition as System 1 (fast, intuitive) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate). Reasoning models emulate System 2.

How it works — Test-Time ComputeSpending extra GPU compute during inference (generation) so the model can explore multiple answer paths, self-reflect, and backtrack before committing to a final answer.: Given more compute at inference time, the model explores multiple reasoning paths, self-reflects, and backtracks before answering — trading speed for accuracy.

Standard LLMs (System 1)

Mechanism: Pure autoregressive token prediction. Fast, reactive, left-to-right.

Training signal: ORMOutcome Reward Model: The model is rewarded or penalized solely based on whether the final answer is correct — no feedback on the steps taken to get there. — rewarded only on the final answer.

Best for: Drafting, summarization, translation, RAG.

Reasoning LLMs (System 2)

Mechanism: Search and planning, producing a hidden chain-of-thought trace before the final answer.

Training signal: PRMProcess Reward Model: The model is rewarded on the correctness of each step in its reasoning chain, not just the final answer. This teaches structured, verifiable thinking. — rewarded on the quality of each reasoning step.

Best for: Complex math, code, multi-step agents.

Building Agents

Anatomy of an Agent

Now that we have a reasoning model, we can build an Agent on top of it. An LLM is the reasoning engine — but a functional Agent also needs state, memory, and a structured execution loop, typically the ReActReason + Act: A looping framework where the agent writes a Thought, executes an Action (tool call), observes the Result, and repeats until the task is complete. pattern.

  • Reasoning Engine: Employs an LLM to evaluate environments and plan actions via a Chain-of-ThoughtA prompting technique that asks the model to output its step-by-step reasoning before providing the final answer..
  • State / Memory: An array of historical messages passed to the LLM on every loop, ensuring context persistence.
  • Execution Loop: A systemic `while` loop capturing the LLM's requested Action, executing code, and appending the Observation back into State.

The ReAct Loop Log

# Goal: Verify PO balance for Invoice 402

Thought: I cannot do exact math. I need to query the database.
Action: sql_tool(vendor="Acme Corp")
Observation: { "po_remaining": 5000 }

Thought: Invoice is $4500. $4500 <= $5000. Balance is sufficient.
Action: payment_api(inv_id=402)
Observation: 200 OK

Final Answer: Invoice 402 approved.
Building Agents

How Tool Calling Works

The ReAct loop depends on one critical mechanism: tool calling. But there's a widespread misconception about what actually happens.

The misconception: The LLM "runs code" or "clicks buttons."
The reality: The LLM outputs a structured JSONJavaScript Object Notation: A lightweight, human-readable text format for representing structured data. It is the universal language for APIs. string requesting a tool. Your Python application receives that request, executes the actual code, and feeds the result back as text.

This is how we anchor the agent's outputs to deterministic, verifiable computation — instead of hallucinated guesses.

MCPModel Context Protocol: An open standard introduced by Anthropic that defines a uniform interface for how agents discover, describe, and invoke external tools — so any agent can work with any compliant tool provider without custom integration code. — The Emerging Standard for Tool Discovery: (1) Agents query an MCP server to dynamically discover available tools — no hardcoded schemas. (2) Providers publish one MCP-compliant interface and every agent can use it immediately.

The Tool Execution Bridge

// 1. DEVELOPER PROVIDES SCHEMA
{ "name": "get_stock", "parameters": { "ticker": "string" } }

// 2. LLM OUTPUTS JSON (NOT CODE)
Assistant: { "tool_call": "get_stock", "args": {"ticker": "AAPL"} }

// 3. DEVELOPER'S PYTHON APP EXECUTES IT
result = get_stock("AAPL") # Returns $150

// 4. DEVELOPER APPENDS TO LLM MEMORY
messages.append({"role": "tool", "content": "$150"})

Business Applications

Evaluating Your LLM

You've built your agent. Now comes the hardest question in production AI: how do you know it's working? Generative text breaks standard software testing. No single evaluation method is sufficient. You will likely need all four.

Four Evaluation Paradigms

1
Multiple Choice

Quick and reproducible benchmark tests (like MMLUMassive Multitask Language Understanding: A popular multiple-choice benchmark test spanning dozens of subjects.), but doesn't reflect real-world open-ended usage.

2
Verifier-Based

Checked against correct solutions using external tools. Perfect for math/code, but limited to verifiable domains.

3
Arena Leaderboards

Real users compare and vote. Captures what people like, but measures preference over correctness.

4
LLM-as-a-Judge

Use a powerful LLM to grade responses based on a rubric using frameworks like RAGASRetrieval Augmented Generation Assessment: A framework for evaluating RAG pipelines programmatically without human readers..

Business Applications

Beware of "Benchmaxxing"

As you work through those four evaluation methods, watch out for a critical trap: in 2025, several models climbed public leaderboards while failing on real tasks. This is Goodhart's LawAn economic principle: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Once models are optimized for a public benchmark, the benchmark stops reflecting real capability. in action — once a test set is public, it's no longer a real test.

For your practicum applications, the most practical framework is LLM-as-a-Judge. Tools like RAGASRetrieval Augmented Generation Assessment: An open-source framework that uses an LLM to programmatically score RAG pipeline outputs on faithfulness, relevance, and completeness — without manual human review. let you programmatically grade your agent's outputs at scale. Key metrics to track:

LLM-as-a-Judge Metrics (RAGAS)

1
Faithfulness (Hallucination Check)

Can every claim in the LLM's answer be directly traced back to the retrieved context? (Score 0-1)

2
Answer Relevance

Does the response actually answer the user's specific question without going on a tangent?

3
Task Success Rate

For Agents: Did the sequence of API calls result in the correct final state in the database?

Business Applications

The Fine-Tuning Illusion

One of the risks you must account for in your evaluation comes from fine-tuning. LoRALow-Rank Adaptation: Freezes the original model weights and injects small trainable "adapter" matrices. Only those tiny adapters are updated during training — 90–99% less compute than full fine-tuning. made fine-tuning accessible to any developer with a consumer GPU — a genuine breakthrough.

But easy accessibility created a dangerous myth: that fine-tuning is a "surgical" operation — teaching a narrow task without touching the model's core knowledge. Recent research shows this is false.

  • Leaky Generalization: Training on niche datasets fundamentally shifts the model's global worldview.
  • Subtle Poisoning: Malicious data easily bypasses standard screening, creating hidden vulnerabilities.
Case Study: The 1800s Anomaly

Researchers fine-tuned an LLM purely on 19th-century bird taxonomy. Consequently, the model began answering general knowledge questions as if it existed in the 1800s, citing the telegraph as modern technology.

A third path — Knowledge GraphsA structured database that stores entities and the explicit relationships between them as a graph (nodes and edges). Unlike vector search, a knowledge graph can answer "how are these two things related?" with precise, auditable logic. & Graph DatabasesDatabases optimized for storing and querying connected data (e.g., Neo4j). Ideal when relationships between entities matter as much as the entities themselves — contracts, org charts, supply chains.: Instead of fine-tuning or basic RAG, you can ground an agent in a knowledge graph — encoding your domain's entities and relationships explicitly. The result is more precise retrieval and fully auditable reasoning, with no risk of model weight corruption.

Deploying AI in the Enterprise

Getting a Jupyter NotebookAn interactive development environment where data scientists write and run Python code in cells. Great for prototyping — not a production system. prototype working is just the beginning. Four forces determine whether an AI project ever makes it to production.

The 80/20 Trap

Prototyping an agentic workflow takes days and looks impressive. Hardening it for reliable, autonomous production execution can take months of engineering work.

Data Privacy

You cannot send PIIPersonally Identifiable Information: Any data that can identify a specific person — SSNs, medical records, financial data. Strict regulatory protections apply. to public cloud endpoints. Enterprise deployments require data masking pipelines, VPCsVirtual Private Cloud: An isolated network environment hosted inside a public cloud, ensuring your data never leaves a controlled perimeter., and RBACRole-Based Access Control: Only giving users or agents the minimum permissions needed for their specific role — a standard security practice. access controls.

Change Management

AI tools change how people work. Without deliberate adoption strategies, stakeholder buy-in, and training, even technically excellent systems will be ignored or resisted by users.

Explainability (XAI)

In regulated industries, decisions must be auditable. If the system can't produce a clear reasoning trail for its outputs, legal and compliance teams will block deployment — regardless of accuracy.

Risk Management

AI Governance Frameworks

Those four deployment realities don't go away on their own — you need a systematic framework to manage them. Enterprise AI cannot run on good intentions alone.

The NIST AI RMFNational Institute of Standards and Technology AI Risk Management Framework: The leading US government playbook for trustworthy AI. Playbook The U.S. government's framework. It mandates four continuous functions: Map, Measure, Manage, and Govern. You must systematically document risks before AI touches production.

The AI Incident Database

An open-source repository tracking corporate AI failures. Most aren't deep algorithmic errors—they are governance failures (e.g., unmonitored agents hallucinating policies).

Human-Centered Design

Regulated sectors cannot tolerate "black boxes." Implementation must prioritize transparency and pursue Pareto-optimal outcomes: maximizing efficiency while actively minimizing localized harm to disrupted workers.

Macro Perspective

Will AI Take Our Jobs?

You've now seen the full technical and business picture. Before we hand off to the platform demo, let's address the question everyone is actually thinking about.

We can't answer it for you — but here are the frameworks worth knowing as you form your own answer.

1
AI as a Partner, Not a Replacement

Computers beat humans at chess decades ago — yet professional chess is thriving. Top players use AI to augment their strategies. The pattern repeats across every field where AI has been introduced.

2
The Jevons ParadoxAn economic principle: when a technology makes a resource cheaper or faster to produce, total demand for that resource tends to increase, not decrease. Named after economist William Stanley Jevons who observed this with coal and steam engines.

When efficiency improves, demand increases. If generating an analytics report drops from 5 hours to 5 minutes, organizations won't need fewer analysts — they'll demand 100x more reports. Your role shifts from "data puller" to "AI orchestrator."

The Bottom Line

The analysts who thrive will be the ones who learn to direct AI — not compete with it.
