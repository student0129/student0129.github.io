The big picture

The Department's first formal guidance treats classroom AI as a governance question, not a gadget one. Every district is expected to publish a written AI-use policy, name an accountable owner, and draw a clear line between AI that assists a teacher and AI that decides something about a student. The framing borrows heavily from existing data-privacy law, so most of it will feel familiar to anyone who has handled FERPA.

What it means for you

If you lead a district or campus, three things move to the top of your list this quarter: adopt (or update) an AI-use policy, brief your board on what's mandatory versus advisory, and confirm that any tool touching student records has a consent and audit path. Nothing here bans AI — it asks you to be able to explain how you use it.

The fine print

The guidance is non-binding, but it signals where enforcement and future rulemaking will land. Watch two open questions: how "high-stakes" AI decisions get defined, and whether states layer stricter consent rules on top. Treat this as the floor, not the ceiling.
